A global nation, cast in color.

Omar Chacón builds his paintings from thousands of hand-cast acrylic drips — each one peeled, sorted, and collaged by hand. A brush is never used.

Selected work

The drips, gathered.

Concentric ovals, lozenges, and discs — assembled into dense, jewel-toned fields. Hover any piece to feel it lift off the canvas.

The method

Three days, no brush.

  1. 01

    Pour

    Acrylic is poured onto wax paper — thousands of brightly colored ovals, drips, and dots, each one allowed to find its own edge.

  2. 02

    Peel

    Once dry, every shape is hand-peeled from the paper. Each becomes a unique, pliable "pre-made brushstroke" — no two alike.

  3. 03

    Gather

    The drips migrate to the canvas and are layered into mosaic-like fields — a social gathering of disparate elements brought into one.

About

From Bogotá to a Queens studio.

Omar Chacón was born in Bogotá, Colombia in 1979 and moved to the United States with his family in 1989. He earned a BFA from Ringling College of Art and Design in 2002 and an MFA in painting from the San Francisco Art Institute in 2004. He lives and works in Queens, New York.

His signature method began with a memory: his self-taught grandfather painting small abstract dots. Those dots became colorful lozenges and discs — read as aerial views of people, mixing, multiplying, and taking over the surface. The work draws on the folk art and indigenous textiles of South America and the mestizo fusion of Latin American life, with recent series turning to the imperial purples and golds of Ancient Rome.

Chacón is represented by Margaret Thatcher Projects (New York), Fouladi Projects (San Francisco), Robischon Gallery (Denver), and Brunnhofer Galerie (Linz, Austria).
